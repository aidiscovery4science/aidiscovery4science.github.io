AI Driven Discovery Systems in Science{%- else -%}{{ site.title }}{%- endif %}

{{ page.subtitle }}



Modern AI systems for scientific discovery, autonomous experimentation, and lab-in-the-loop platforms increasingly operate in closed feedback loops: propose → evaluate → update → repeat.

Despite this iterative structure, most current systems remain architecturally static and are optimized for short-horizon, one-shot performance rather than sustained, recursive improvement. Their update mechanisms are typically limited to parameter optimization under fixed architectures, with little capacity for structural adaptation, memory consolidation, or strategy evolution.

This workshop introduces a unifying perspective: Self-improving AI systems can be understood as instances of sequential experimental design in which the object of experimentation is the system itself. AI-driven discovery is an ongoing dialogue between a system and its environment, where the system progressively refines not only its hypotheses, but the mechanisms by which it generates them.

We aim to establish a shared conceptual framework for recursive scientific systems, systems that improve through sustained interaction with noisy, expensive, real-world evaluators (e.g., wet labs, robotics platforms, or compute-constrained simulations).

Key questions the workshop aims to address:

  • Is structural plasticity necessary for sustained improvement?
  • Can growth, pruning, and consolidation policies be formalized as experimental interventions?
  • What role does evolution play alongside gradient-based optimization?
  • Is open-endedness required for scientific discovery?
  • How can recursive systems remain stable under noisy, sparse, expensive feedback?
  • What lessons transfer between digital discovery (algorithms, models, code) and physical discovery (proteins, materials, robotics)?

The workshop will be held at Villa Elisabeth in Berlin, Germany.
